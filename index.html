1. What Is Property Conveyancing?

Property conveyancing is the legal mechanism that facilitates the transfer of property ownership from a seller (vendor) to a buyer (purchaser). The term "conveyancing" originates from the old English word "convey," meaning to transfer. In modern Australian real estate, it encompasses a series of legal, administrative, and financial steps that must be completed before ownership can legally change hands.

Legal Definition (Victoria): Under the Sale of Land Act 1962 (Vic), conveyancing involves preparing, executing, verifying, and lodging legal documents to effect the transfer of property ownership.

The process begins when a buyer makes an offer (usually through a Contract of Sale) and continues until settlement, when the buyer receives the keys and the seller receives payment. A licensed conveyancer or property lawyer manages this process, ensuring all legal requirements are met and protecting their client's interests.

Key Components of Conveyancing

  • Contract preparation and review
  • Title searches and verification
  • Property adjustments (rates, taxes)
  • Finance coordination
  • Settlement coordination
  • Post-settlement notifications

In Victoria and Queensland, conveyancing is regulated by state-specific legislation, but the core principles remain consistent across Australia. The process typically takes 30–90 days, depending on contract conditions and complexity.

3. Conveyancing Process for Buyers: Step-by-Step

Buyers typically engage a conveyancer before signing a Contract of Sale. This allows for pre-contract review and identification of issues early.

1

Pre-Contract Review

Your conveyancer examines the Section 32 Vendor Statement (VIC) or Contract of Sale (QLD) for:

  • Zoning restrictions
  • Outgoings (rates, body corporate)
  • Easements and covenants
  • Building permits
  • Planning overlays
2

Contract Exchange

Once satisfied, you sign the contract and pay the deposit (usually 10%). The contract becomes binding, triggering cooling-off periods (if applicable).

3

Due Diligence Period

Your conveyancer orders searches:

  • Title Search
  • Council Certificates
  • Land Tax
  • Water Authority
  • Contamination Reports
4

Finance Approval

Coordinate with your lender for unconditional finance approval. Your conveyancer liaises with the bank to prepare mortgage documents.

5

Pre-Settlement Inspection

Conduct final walkthrough to ensure property condition matches contract terms. Report any issues immediately.

6

Settlement

Funds transferred, ownership changes, keys released. Your conveyancer handles all paperwork electronically via PEXA.

Pro Tip: In Victoria, buyers have a 3-day cooling-off period for residential properties (excluding auctions). Use this time wisely for building inspections and finance confirmation.

4. Conveyancing Process for Sellers

Sellers should engage a conveyancer before listing to prepare the Contract of Sale and Section 32 (VIC).

Seller's Key Responsibilities

  1. Prepare Section 32/Vendor Statement – Must disclose all material facts
  2. Order certificates early – Avoid delays
  3. Calculate adjustments – Rates, water, land tax
  4. Coordinate vacancy – Property must be empty at settlement
  5. Final meter readings – Electricity, gas, water

Your conveyancer calculates adjustments so you receive the correct net proceeds. For example:

  • Council rates paid to 30 June, settlement on 15 March → Buyer reimburses seller for unused portion
  • Water usage → Final reading determines buyer's payment

8. Essential Property Searches Explained

Searches reveal critical information about the property. Missing one can be catastrophic.

A

Title Search

Confirms ownership, encumbrances, caveats. Cost: $25–$50. Turnaround: 1–2 days.

B

Plan of Subdivision

Shows boundaries, easements. Essential for new developments.

C

Council Property Report

Reveals zoning, overlays, building approvals, flood risk.

D

Land Tax Clearance

Confirms no unpaid land tax (seller's responsibility).

12. Frequently Asked Questions

How long does conveyancing take?

Typically 30–90 days from contract exchange to settlement. The contract specifies the settlement period, with 60 days being most common for established properties.

Can I do conveyancing myself?

Technically yes, but not recommended. DIY conveyancing carries high risks of errors in contracts, searches, or adjustments, potentially costing thousands. Professional conveyancers carry insurance for mistakes.

What is a Section 32 statement?

Required in Victoria, the Section 32 Vendor Statement discloses all material facts about the property including rates, zoning, outgoings, and notices. Sellers must provide this before signing.

Who pays conveyancing fees?

Each party pays their own conveyancer. Buyers pay stamp duty and registration fees. Sellers pay agent commission and adjustment reimbursements.

What happens if settlement is delayed?

The defaulting party pays penalty interest (usually 10–12% p.a.) on the balance from settlement date until actual settlement. Your conveyancer negotiates extensions if needed.
